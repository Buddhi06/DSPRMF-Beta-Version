Phase 2: Step 4:
Benefit Risk Analysis

A benefit-risk analysis should be performed for the residual risks that are judged unacceptable (identified in setp 3) and for which further risk control is not practicable. The benefit-risk analysis is used to determine whether the residual risk is outweighed by the expected benefits of the intended use of the MDS AI model. It is important to note that practicability of further risk reduction should be evaluated before considering a benefit-risk analysis.The MDS AI model should not be placed on the market if the benefits do not outweigh the residual risks or until additional risk reduction measures are applied.

Practicability of further risk control measures can be evaluated by considering the following:

  1. Technical feasibility of the controls - Whether it is possible to implement the control with available technology and resources?
  2. Possibility of arising new risks - Whethere there is a possibility of introducing new risks, following the implementation of controls?
  3. Clinical performance assessment - Would the new control affect diagnosis decisions? Would it delay treatment decisions? Would it affect intended use?

For performing benefit-risk analysis, the following steps need to be followed:

  1. Determine Clinical Benefit
  2. Determine Risk
  3. Compare Benefit-Risk
  4. Determine acceptability

(1) Determine Clinical Benefit

The following factors need to be considered during clinical benefit identification:

Factor Description
The type of expected clinical benefits Clinical benefit is the positive impact of using the MDS AI model on an individual’s health, such as improvements in the quality of life, symptom relief, pain relief, enhanced diagnosis, reducing the probability of death etc...For example, whether the AI model performs a significant role in a life-saving medical device or is essential in a specific medical scenario.
The probability that the patient will experience the expected clinical benefits The likelihood that the MDS AI model is effective in performing the clinical function. The amount of patients experience the benfit should also be considered in probability identification. It is important to note that based on the availability of data it is sometimes possible to predict which patients may experience a benefit, whereas other times this cannot be well predicted. The data may show that a benefit may be experienced only by a small portion of patients in the target population, or, on the other hand, that a benefit may occur frequently in patients throughout the target population. In some cases, the probability of receiving the benefit could be estimated for a whole population, as the ratio of A/B, where A = the estimated number of people in a population (e.g., a country) who would receive the therapy, and B = the estimated number of the people in that population who could benefit from the therapy (e.g., people with the relevant medical condition). This would treat the accessibility of a therapy in a given population as a public health benefit. Moreover, a large benefit experienced by a small proportion of participants may raise different considerations than does a small benefit experienced by a large proportion of participants. For example, a large benefit, even if experienced by a small population, may be significant enough to outweigh risks, whereas a small benefit may not, unless experienced by a large population of participants.
The magnitude of the expected clinical benefits The degree to which the patient will experience the clinical benefit. Determining magnitude values is a more subjective task than determining the probability. FDA always suggest to assess the magnitude of benefits using a predefined scale.
The duration of the expected effects How long the benefit is expected to last for the patient. Some treatments are curative, whereas, some may need to be repeated frequently over the patient’s lifetime. To the extent that it is known, the duration of a treatment’s effect may directly influence how its benefit is defined. Treatments that must be repeated over time may introduce greater risk, or the benefit experienced may diminish each time the treatment is repeated.

The benefit value is quantified based on the Probability of clinical benefit experience and the Magnitude of those expeced clinical benefits.

The following assessment scales can be used to assess the magnitude of the expected clinical benefits:

Assessment Scales - Magnitude of the expected clinical benefits
Qualitative Values Semi-Quantitative Values Description
Very High 96–100 10 The expected clinical benefit is critical or transformative, with a major positive impact on patient outcomes (e.g., life-saving, major improvement in quality of life).
High 80–95 8 The expected clinical benefit is significant, providing clear clinical improvements (e.g., improved diagnosis accuracy, faster treatment, reduced complications).

A magnitude value need to be assigned to the identified benefit based on the above assessment scales. When assigning a value for the magnitude, the aspects mentioned previously under the description of the factors should also be considered.

(1) Semi-Quantitative - Benefit Value Calculation

After identifying the probability and magnitude, the benefit value can be determined using the below formula:

Benefit Value = Probability * Magnitude

Probability – The probability that the patient will experience the expected clinical benefit
Magnitude – The magnitude of the expected clinical benefit

(3) Compare benefit-risk


(1) Determine benefit-risk Ratio - Semi-Quantitative

Once the benefit value and risk values are determined, the benefit-risk ratio can be determined using the formula below:

Benefit-Risk Ratio = Benefit Value/Risk Value

It should be noted that this approach assigns a value to each individual benefit and risk; it does not sum all risks or benefits to obtain a single value for each. This results in the identification of a pairwise comparison between each risk and each benefit. Although summarising benefits and risks to singular values would certainly present a clean summary with a singular benefit-risk ratio, such a method would lose the granularity that allows for an in-depth analysis of benefit and risk.

(4) Determine Acceptability

  • In general, as per the semi-quantitative benefit-risk ratio, any ratio greater than 1 is considered favorable, as the benefits outweigh the risks (i.e., the benefit value is greater than the risk value).
  • As per the qualitative benefit-risk determination benefit-risk pairs highlighted in red color are considered favorable.
  • If analysis determined that the risks are outweighted by the benefits the manufacturer should determine what information should be communicated in labeling to manage this residual risk. Moreover, this needs to be communicated in such a way that it does not provide a blueprint for a potential attacker to exploit the non-mitigated vulnerability.
  • However, a direct comparison of benefit and risk is complicated. Consistent with the requirements of the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR 2017/745) and guidance in ISO 14971 and ISO/TR 24971, the determination of acceptability should also consider the following factors without solely relying on the semi-quantitative or qualitative scales:
    1. The type of the expected benefit as described here
    2. The duration of the expected benefit as described here
    3. State of the art (such as similar Medical Device Software, existing clinical practices, or alternative therapeutic or diagnostic options). Benefit-risk comparisons need to be conducted for existing state-of-the-art technologies, comparable Medical Device Software, and alternative diagnostic or therapeutic pathways where applicable. In general the benefi-risk ratio should be greater for the MDS AI model under consideration than for the state of the art for at least half of the benefit-risk pairs.
    4. Expert judgement by experienced and knowledgeable individuals, usually multidisciplinary team comprising medical, clinical or application experts.
